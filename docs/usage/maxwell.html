Maxwell’s Equations¶

This is the following form of Maxwell’s equations assumed throughout wavesight.

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \nabla\cdot \vec{D} = 0 \\ \nabla \times \vec{H} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\vec{D} \\ \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 \\ \nabla\times{\vec{E}} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \\ \vec{D} = \epsilon_r \vec{E} \\ \vec{H} = \frac{1}{\mu_r} \vec{B} \end{align}\end{split}\]

In these equations, \(\vec{D}\) is the electric displacement field, \(\vec{H}\) is the magnetic field, \(\vec{B}\) is the magnetic flux density, and \(\vec{E}\) is the electric field. \(\epsilon_r\) and \(\mu_r\) are the relative permittivity and permeability of the medium.

These are Maxwell’s equations in SI for a homogeneous linear medium without free charges or current, having set both \(\mu_0\) and \(\epsilon_0\) to 1.

Since here we are only concerned with electromagnetic waves at optical frequencies where matter is mostly magnetically transparent, we can assume \(\mu_r = 1\).
